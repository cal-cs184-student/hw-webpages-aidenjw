Overview

In this homework, we implemented a mass-spring cloth simulator with structural, shearing, and bending springs, Verlet-style numerical integration, collision handling, self-collision, and several GLSL shaders. The simulator models cloth as a grid of point masses connected by constraints, then updates those point masses over time under external forces, damping, collision responses, and spring-length corrections.

The most interesting part of the assignment was seeing how many small modeling choices affect the final motion. Changing spring stiffness, density, damping, collision friction, spatial hashing, or shader normals can make the same cloth look light and fluttery, heavy and sagging, smooth, bumpy, or reflective. This made the project feel like a useful bridge between physical simulation and visual rendering.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration


The spring constant ks controls how stiff the cloth is. When ks is very low, the springs are weak, so the cloth stretches more easily under gravity, sags more, and looks softer and looser as it falls. It tends to deform significantly before reaching its resting state. When ks is very high, the cloth is much stiffer, so it resists stretching, stays more taut, and does not droop as much. In the final resting state, a high-ks cloth keeps its shape better, while a low-ks cloth looks much more flexible and saggy.


Density changes how heavy the cloth is. A higher density means each point mass has more mass, so the cloth falls with more inertia and tends to form deeper folds or stronger wave-like motion before settling. A lower density makes the cloth lighter, so it moves more gently and usually settles with less dramatic motion. Density changes how strongly gravity and inertia affect the cloth’s motion.


Damping controls how quickly the cloth loses energy over time. With low damping, the cloth keeps moving for much longer, so it swings and oscillates noticeably before settling. With high damping, the cloth loses energy more quickly, so it reaches its final resting state faster and with less bouncing. At extremely low damping, the cloth can continue moving for a very long time, while at very high damping it settles much more quickly and appears less lively.


For ks, the most noticeable difference is how much the cloth stretches and sags. In the low-ks image, the cloth appears much softer and droop more heavily. In the high-ks image, the cloth looks more rigid and taut, with much less bending and stretching. These differences are especially noticeable both during the fall and in the final resting state.

For density, the main visible difference is how strongly the cloth responds to gravity and inertia. In the low-density case, the cloth looks lighter and move more gently. In the high-density case, the cloth falls more heavily and may show deeper folds or stronger wave-like motion before settling. These differences are often easiest to see while the cloth is in motion, rather than only at the very end.

For damping, the biggest difference is how long the cloth continues to oscillate. In the low-damping case, the cloth keeps swinging and fluttering for a long time before coming to rest. In the high-damping case, the cloth settles much faster and show far less bouncing. These differences are most noticeable shortly after the simulation starts and during the settling process.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

Implementation of handling collisions with spheres and planes
For sphere collisions, I first computed the vector from the sphere’s center to the point mass and checked whether the point mass was inside or on the sphere. If it was, I normalized that direction vector and used it to find the tangent point on the sphere’s surface. I then computed the correction vector from the point mass’s last_position to that tangent point, and updated the point mass’s new position by moving it along that correction vector scaled by (1 - friction). This bumps the point mass back onto the sphere’s surface while accounting for friction.

For plane collisions, I checked whether the point mass crossed the plane during the current timestep by comparing the signs of the dot products from the current and previous positions against the plane normal. If the signs differed, then the point mass crossed the plane. I computed the tangent point by intersecting the line segment from last_position to position with the plane. Then I offset that tangent point slightly along the plane normal using SURFACE_OFFSET so that the point mass would end up just above the plane on the same side it started from. Finally, I computed the correction vector from last_position to this offset tangent point and updated the position using the correction scaled by (1 - friction).

In Cloth::simulate, after Verlet integration, I iterated through every point mass and tested it against every collision object in the scene by calling obj->collide(pm). This ensures that all point masses respond to both spheres and planes before the spring-length correction step.

Shaded cloth from scene/sphere.json in its final resting state on the sphere using the default ks = 5000 as well as with ks = 500 and ks = 50000.
When the cloth is simulated on the sphere with a low spring constant such as ks = 500, it behaves like a much softer material. It stretches more easily and droops further around the sphere, so the cloth conforms more closely to the sphere’s shape and hangs lower between pinned points. With the default ks = 5000, the cloth still drapes naturally over the sphere, but it does not stretch as dramatically and looks more balanced between flexibility and stiffness.

With a very high spring constant such as ks = 50000, the cloth is much stiffer and resists stretching. As a result, it does not wrap around the sphere as tightly, and its final resting shape appears more taut and elevated. Overall, increasing ks makes the cloth look less saggy and more rigid, while decreasing ks makes the cloth look softer and more elastic.

The SURFACE_OFFSET keeps the cloth slightly above the plane, which helps avoid numerical issues and prevents the cloth from appearing to clip into the surface. In the final resting state, the cloth lies flat against the plane.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

As by the instructions, I implemented self-collision using spatial hashing so the algorithm would run in linear time rather than O(n^2). Every time we simulate the scene, after the Verlet integration updates the point masses, Cloth::simulate calls build_spatial_map(). This resets the previous timestamp's hash map, then places every point mass in a 3 dimentional spatial bucket based on its current position. hash_position() converts each point mass position into integer cell cooridates by dividing teh bucket dimensions and taking the floor of the results. These cell coordinates are combined to create a single hash key, which is used to store the point mass in my hash map. In the simulation step, we call self_collide() on all of these point masses. This looks up other masses in the same spatial bucket, checking whether neighbors are closer than twice the thickness (if they are touching). If so, it computes a vector to correct this, pushing the point mass away from its neighbor so the pair remain at least twice the thickness apart. All these correction vectors for each point mass are acumulated and averaged.

Part 5: Shaders

Shader Programs

A shader is a program that uses the GPU and is part of the graphics pipeline. Instead of using the CPU to compute the every pixel's color, we send mesh data, uniforms, and textures to the GPU all at once, which runs the code in parallel. Vertex shaders run once per vertex. They take the position, normal, tangent, and UV coordinates, transforming them into the screen space, and passing the interpolated data to the next stage. Fragment shaders then run for each rasterized fragment or for every potential pixel. They use the interpolated world position, normal, UVs, textures, and other information to compute the final output color. Togther they are used to create lighting and material effects. On a higher level, this can be thought of as first placing the objects on the scene, then deciding how visible each point on the objects should look.

Blinn Phong Shading

Blinn Phong shading is a local lighting model that approximates how a surface interacts with light. It consists of ambient, diffuse, and specular light. The ambient factor is the constant base illumination, representing indirect light in the sun. This is mainly so surfaces facign away from the light aren't completely black. The diffuse term models the matte reflection. It's dependent on angles between the surface normal and light direction, so the more the surface faces the light, the brighter that surface will be. The specular term models the shinyness of the surface. This is done through a halfway vector between the light direction and the view direction. The more the surface normal aligns with this halfway vector, the shinier the surface appears.

Extra Credit Shader Images for part 5

For the extra credit shader, I implemented a Fresnel style effect. I did this by measuring the angle between the surface normal and the camera direction. The dot product of the normal and viewing direction changes depending if the surface is facing towards the camera or to the side, which is used to find the fresnel value. I then passed this value into a sine based function to generate RGB colors depending on the angle. This is what gives the rainbow like effect.

Bump and Displacement Mapping

Bump and displacement mapping both utilize height mapps, but the main difference is bump mapping only changes the normals used for lighting. This makes the geometry stay the same, so the surface looks bumpy but the silhouette and shadows remain unchanged. Displacement mapping moves the vertices along their normals using the height map, changing the geometry of the surface. This makes the surface and silhouette look more detailed and have more texture. It's kind of difficult to see the difference in the images, but if we look closely at the spheres, we can see the silhouette of the bump mapped sphere is perfectly round, while the displacement sphere has a slightly jagged silhouette. The difference can more easily be seen in the exaggerated displacement sphere.

Part 6: Extra Credit - Additional cloth simulation features!

For the extra credit, I implemented a spatially varying wind force to the simulation. The wind force changes based on each point mass's current position and the surface normal. This was done by computing a fixed wind direction and a sinusoidal gust value based on the position. This makes different parts of the same surface/cloth experience varying wind strengths, which is why the screenshots below appear to show the cloth being blown away realistically. To more easily see the effects of the wind, I disabled gravity for the majority of the screenshots, so the only force on the cloth is the wind. In one screenshot I enabled gravity and let the cloth fall on the sphere. We can easily see the differences in this compared to the original simulation, as the cloth doesn't land on the center of the sphere and instead gets blown to the side.
