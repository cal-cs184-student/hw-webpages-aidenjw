Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration


The spring constant ks controls how stiff the cloth is. When ks is very low, the springs are weak, so the cloth stretches more easily under gravity, sags more, and looks softer and looser as it falls. It tends to deform significantly before reaching its resting state. When ks is very high, the cloth is much stiffer, so it resists stretching, stays more taut, and does not droop as much. In the final resting state, a high-ks cloth keeps its shape better, while a low-ks cloth looks much more flexible and saggy.


Density changes how heavy the cloth is. A higher density means each point mass has more mass, so the cloth falls with more inertia and tends to form deeper folds or stronger wave-like motion before settling. A lower density makes the cloth lighter, so it moves more gently and usually settles with less dramatic motion. Density changes how strongly gravity and inertia affect the cloth’s motion.


Damping controls how quickly the cloth loses energy over time. With low damping, the cloth keeps moving for much longer, so it swings and oscillates noticeably before settling. With high damping, the cloth loses energy more quickly, so it reaches its final resting state faster and with less bouncing. At extremely low damping, the cloth can continue moving for a very long time, while at very high damping it settles much more quickly and appears less lively.


For ks, the most noticeable difference is how much the cloth stretches and sags. In the low-ks image, the cloth appears much softer and droop more heavily. In the high-ks image, the cloth looks more rigid and taut, with much less bending and stretching. These differences are especially noticeable both during the fall and in the final resting state.

For density, the main visible difference is how strongly the cloth responds to gravity and inertia. In the low-density case, the cloth looks lighter and move more gently. In the high-density case, the cloth falls more heavily and may show deeper folds or stronger wave-like motion before settling. These differences are often easiest to see while the cloth is in motion, rather than only at the very end.

For damping, the biggest difference is how long the cloth continues to oscillate. In the low-damping case, the cloth keeps swinging and fluttering for a long time before coming to rest. In the high-damping case, the cloth settles much faster and show far less bouncing. These differences are most noticeable shortly after the simulation starts and during the settling process.
